Clarify your purpose
What is this piece really trying to do? Go beyond the format and name the outcome. Are you informing? Persuading? Preparing someone to act?

Why it matters
A clear goal helps you write faster, get better suggestions, and avoid fuzzy feedback later.

Coaching cue
Ask:
“What do I want the reader to understand, feel, or do after reading this?”

Examples

  • A one-pager to help our VP prep for a client meeting
  • A proposal to convince the team to shift priorities
  • A briefing that explains our policy shift to partners

Identify your real audience
Who will actually read this? Not just their title — but their perspective, priorities, and what they already know (or assume). Be specific.

Why it matters
Knowing your audience helps you choose the right tone, examples, and level of detail. It makes your writing land.

Coaching cue
Ask:
“What does this reader need from me? What might they push back on?”

Examples

  • A new team lead unfamiliar with our legacy process
  • Senior executives focused on policy risk
  • External partners with limited technical background

Choose the tone that fits your audience
Tone shapes how your message lands. A formal tone signals professionalism and authority. A conversational tone may build rapport and clarity. Business casual often strikes a balance.

Why it matters
Your tone should match the relationship. A legal update, a team check-in, and a proposal to a skeptical audience might all require different voices.

Coaching cue
Ask:
“What tone will help this audience hear and trust what I’m saying?”

Examples

  • Business formal — An external policy memo to partners or regulators
  • Business casual — An internal update to managers across departments
  • Conversational — A quick explainer for peers or a first-draft concept note
  • Or describe your tone below — e.g., encouraging, candid, measured, supportive
